Our approach to screen-free learning, child safety, and responsible technology — grounded in research, guided by care.

Why this matters

Children deserve technology designed with their development in mind.

Most technology built for children is not designed for children. It is designed for engagement metrics, advertising revenue, and retention curves. The incentive structures of consumer technology reward products that capture attention — not products that nurture development. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends avoiding screen-based media for children under 18 months and limiting use to one hour of high-quality programming for children ages 2 to 5. The reality is that the average preschooler in America now exceeds those guidelines by a factor of three.

This is not a failure of parenting. It is a failure of design. When the most well-funded, most sophisticated product teams in the world optimize for time-on-screen, even the most attentive families find themselves outmatched. The problem is structural, and the solution must be structural too.

Learn with Mochi exists because we believe technology can be genuinely beneficial for the humans who matter most — children. We are a public-benefit corporation. That is not a marketing label. It is a legal structure that encodes our commitment to child wellbeing into our corporate charter, with the same weight as our obligation to shareholders. When the interests of growth and the interests of children conflict, our legal structure requires us to choose children.

Our principles

Five pillars that guide everything we build.

Screen-free by conviction, not by trend.

We are not anti-technology. We are pro-appropriate-technology. The research on screen time before age six is unambiguous: studies consistently link early excessive screen exposure to delayed speech development, reduced attention span, and limited social engagement. But the answer is not no technology — it is the right technology. Physical. Tactile. Multi-sensory. The kind that engages the whole body, not just the eyes and fingertips. Our wooden coding blocks, fabric adventure maps, and physical programming board are designed to meet children where developmental science says they learn best: through their hands.

Montessori-grounded, science-validated.

Maria Montessori's insights about self-directed learning, tactile exploration, and sequential thinking have been validated by over a century of developmental psychology and modern neuroscience. Motor engagement during learning strengthens memory formation. Haptic feedback improves spatial reasoning. Self-paced exploration builds executive function. Our wooden blocks, fabric maps, and physical programming board are not nostalgic design choices — they are neurological tools, each one informed by research on how young minds construct knowledge through sensory experience.

Story-based, not screen-based.

Children construct meaning through narrative. This is not pedagogical theory — it is how the developing brain organizes information, assigns emotional significance, and builds long-term memory. Every Mochi adventure is designed around a story arc: a character, a journey, a problem to solve. Coding concepts — sequences, loops, functions, decomposition — are embedded in the narrative, not bolted on. A child does not "do a coding exercise." They help Mochi explore the solar system, navigate an ancient forest, or build a robot city. The code is how they get there.

Open-ended play, no fail states.

There is no score in Mochi. No timer. No "wrong answer." The system is designed for divergent thinking and creative exploration. A child who codes the rover in an unexpected direction has not failed — they have discovered something. This design philosophy aligns with decades of research on growth mindset and intrinsic motivation: when children are freed from the anxiety of evaluation, they experiment more boldly, persist longer, and develop deeper conceptual understanding. Play is not the opposite of learning. It is the mechanism of it.

Inclusive by design, accessible by mission.

Mochi arrives as a blank slate. No gendered marketing. No cultural assumptions baked into the product. No language barriers in the physical play. Our kits ship to 62 countries across six continents because computational thinking is a universal literacy, not a privilege reserved for children in well-funded school districts. As a public-benefit corporation, expanding access is encoded in our legal charter — not just our marketing copy. Every design decision we make is tested against a simple question: does this work for every child?

Your family's privacy

No accounts. No tracking. No data collection.

Mochi requires zero internet connection, zero app downloads, and zero account creation. There is no telemetry. No analytics. No behavioral data collected from children. The programming board reads wooden blocks via physical contact — no Bluetooth, no WiFi, no cloud processing. Your child's play stays in your living room.

We believe the best data policy is collecting no data at all. In an era when children's digital footprints are monetized before they can spell their own names, we made a deliberate architectural decision: Mochi has no digital footprint because Mochi has no digital connection.

This approach exceeds the requirements of the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) — not by implementing better consent flows or more secure databases, but by eliminating the need for them entirely. There is nothing to breach because there is nothing to collect. Privacy is not a feature we added. It is a consequence of how we built the product from the ground up.

Our long-term vision

Technology should serve childhood, not consume it.

We imagine a future where every educational technology is designed with the same care we bring to Mochi. Where "safe for children" means more than content filters and parental controls — it means tools that develop agency, creativity, and genuine understanding. Where the measure of a product is not how long a child uses it, but how much they grow from it.

The world does not need more software that teaches children to swipe. It needs tools that teach them to think, to build, to question, and to create. That is the standard we hold ourselves to — and the standard we believe every company building for children should be held to as well. Not because it is good marketing. Because it is the right thing to do.
