Somewhere between the first iPad and the first algorithm, we forgot that. Learn with Mochi exists to remember.

The world children inherit

The most powerful technologies in history are being handed to children as black boxes.

Today's devices are sleek, polished, and sealed shut. They "just work." And that's the problem. When technology feels like magic, children become consumers of it — not creators. The question isn't whether they'll use technology. It's whether they'll understand it, question it, and shape it — or just swipe.

What if we opened a window?

What we believe

Technology is too important to be limited to the 0.01% creating it.

More creators, not consumers

We believe the next generation should be makers, not just users. That coding is a language — one that should be as natural to learn as ABCs and 123s.

Hands-on, or nothing at all

Every VC told us to build an app. But delivering a screen-free solution through a screen was fighting the enemy with the enemy's own weapons. It had to be tactile, physical, real.

The Montessori method, applied to AI literacy

Maria Montessori proved over 100 years ago that children learn through touch, sequence, and self-directed discovery. We built on that foundation.

Our story

How it started

Recreating childhood magic: one bear, one family, one child at a time.

Ukraine, 1990s

A grandmother's apartment in post-Soviet Ukraine.

Mark grew up taking apart rusty electronics with his babusya. She taught him that nothing was ever broken — things were just waiting for someone with imagination to turn them into what could be.

Canada, 1990s

A new country, a new language, a borrowed home.

Daria arrived in Canada from China barely able to speak English. A family of refugees from the former Yugoslavia took her in. They taught her that generosity can exist even when we have so little. Especially when we have so little.

San Francisco, 2018

Every VC said no.

"Make it an app," they said. "Software scales." So they built it themselves. First blocks: laser-cut on plywood from Home Depot. First audio: recorded on a phone. First factory: months living in Shenzhen.

2019

Hundreds of hours of testing.

Prototyped by finding family friends willing to host in-home tests. Spent hundreds of hours designing, prototyping, testing, and collecting feedback from dozens of parents, kids, teachers, and families.

Christmas 2019

Promise kept.

720 Kickstarter backers. $218,000 raised in 38 days. Every single kit shipped on time for Christmas. Mochi's first adventure had begun.

Common Questions

Your questions, answered

From parents, grandparents, and educators exploring Learn with Mochi.

Learn with Mochi is a screen-free coding robotics kit designed for children ages 3 to 9. It combines a cuddly Mochi Bear plush, a LEGO-compatible rover, natural wood coding blocks, and story-driven adventure packs to teach computational thinking through hands-on play. Children learn real programming concepts like sequences, loops, functions, and debugging — all without a screen, app, or download. It's Montessori-approved and ready to play straight out of the box.
Mochi uses physical wooden coding blocks that children place on a screenless programming board. Each block represents a command — move forward, turn left, loop, or call a function. The board reads the sequence and sends instructions to the rover, which executes the code in real time. Audio feedback from original songs and storytelling narration guides the experience. It's tangible, multi-sensory, and grounded in the Montessori principle that children learn best through touch and self-directed discovery.
Learn with Mochi is the only screen-free coding kit that combines a loveable character, story-driven learning, LEGO compatibility, and Montessori-approved natural wood materials in a single system. Unlike Botley (which is button-programmed) or Cubetto (which has no narrative layer), Mochi wraps coding concepts in rich adventures — 16 themed story packs covering everything from planets and biology to neuroscience and dinosaurs. Each adventure builds on prior knowledge, creating a growing curriculum rather than a standalone toy. The rover is also LEGO-compatible, meaning children can customize and extend their builds infinitely.
Absolutely. Learn with Mochi is used in classrooms and homeschool settings across 62 countries. The kit includes STEM certifications and supports educator professional development. Because it's screen-free and self-contained, there's no setup, IT support, or device management required — children can begin coding within minutes. The 16 adventure themes map to real curriculum areas including math, science, literacy, and computational thinking, making it easy for teachers to integrate into existing lesson plans.

Learn with Mochi was co-founded in 2018 by Mark Pavlyukovskyy and Daria Zhao. Mark, a Ukrainian immigrant and Forbes 30 Under 30 honoree, previously built the Piper Computer Kit (100,000+ units shipped to 70 countries). Daria, a Chinese-Canadian immigrant and former Wall Street analyst, left finance to teach STEM classes in San Francisco. They saw a generation of children growing up with polished devices that masked how technology actually works — and set out to build a hands-on, screen-free way for the youngest learners to become creators of technology, not just consumers. Learn with Mochi is a public-benefit corporation dedicated to the long-term educational wellbeing of children worldwide.
