Learn with Mochi is a public-benefit corporation founded in 2018. We build the Montessori method for AI literacy and computational thinking — one bear, one family, one child at a time.

The world we see

A generation is growing up inside sealed machines.

In 2007, the iPhone was introduced. By 2012, the iPad had become the default babysitter. By 2018 — when we founded this company — the average American child was spending over four hours a day on screens before the age of two.

These devices are miracles of engineering. They are also perfectly sealed black boxes. Unlike the clunky Commodore 64 that invited tinkering, or the Soviet-era radios that a curious child in Ukraine could take apart with a screwdriver, today's technology masks its own inner workings. It "just works." And that is precisely the problem.

When technology feels like magic, children learn to consume it. When it feels like a language — something with grammar, logic, and creative possibility — children learn to create with it. The difference between those two outcomes will define the next century.

Technology is too important to be limited to the 0.01% creating it in Silicon Valley.

We believe computational thinking is a foundational literacy — as essential as reading, writing, and arithmetic. And we believe it should begin not at age 12, in a computer lab, but at age 3, on a living room floor, with wooden blocks and a curious bear.

Where we come from

Two immigrants, one question.

Mark Pavlyukovskyy grew up in post-Soviet Ukraine, in his grandmother's apartment, taking apart rusty electronics. His babusya taught him that nothing was ever broken — things were just waiting for someone with imagination to turn them into what could be. He went on to study molecular biology at Princeton, then biomedical engineering at Oxford as an NIH Scholar. A medical diagnosis during his first year compressed his sense of time and made him ask: what can I build that will outlast me?

His answer was Play Piper — a computer kit that shipped over 100,000 units to children in 70+ countries, earned endorsements from Steve Wozniak, and landed him on Forbes 30 Under 30 in Education. But Piper was for older children. The question that haunted Mark was: what about the youngest ones?

Daria Zhao arrived in Canada from China as a child, barely able to speak English. A family of refugees from the former Yugoslavia took her in while her parents enrolled in language classes and worked odd jobs. They taught her that generosity can exist even when we have so little. Especially when we have so little.

Daria built a career on Wall Street, then walked away from it. She moved to San Francisco and began teaching STEM classes three times a week at the Potrero Hill Neighborhood House — trading her time for free office space. She discovered something that every parent knows but few curricula acknowledge: children of every age learn best when they can touch things.

In 2018, Mark and Daria connected. They saw the same gap: a generation growing up with polished devices that masked how technology actually works. And they asked the same question: What if we gave children the tools to build the future, not just consume it?

The hard choice

Every VC said no.

"Make it an app," they said on Sand Hill Road. "Software scales. Hardware doesn't. The returns aren't there."

But Mark and Daria were adamant. A pure software product meant delivering their screen-free solution through the very enemy they were fighting: screen time. The whole point was that children should hold something real. Feel the weight of a wooden block. Hear the click of a rover coming to life. Watch their code execute in physical space, not behind glass.

It had to be hands-on. Or nothing at all.

So they built it themselves. The first coding blocks were laser-cut on plywood from Home Depot. The first audio files were recorded on Daria's phone. The first user tests happened in family friends' living rooms. They spent months living in Shenzhen, learning manufacturing from the ground up.

On October 16, 2019, they launched on Kickstarter. They were funded in under 12 hours. 720 backers pledged $218,000 — over four times their goal. And on Christmas morning 2019, every single kit arrived on time.

What we stand for

Four convictions that shape everything we build.

Hands touch what screens can't.

Maria Montessori proved over a century ago that children construct knowledge through their senses — through touch, sequence, repetition, and self-correction. Neuroscience has since confirmed it: motor engagement during learning strengthens memory formation and conceptual understanding. Our coding blocks are made from natural wood. Our adventure maps are fabric you unfold on the floor. Our rover moves through real space. None of this is nostalgic. It is neurological.

Stories outlast features.

Children don't remember specifications. They remember characters, journeys, and the feeling of discovery. Every Mochi adventure is built around narrative: a curious bear, a new world to explore, a problem to solve. The coding concepts — sequences, loops, functions, decomposition — are embedded in the story, not bolted on. A child doesn't "learn to code." They help Mochi travel the solar system, and the code is how they get there.

Inclusion is the point.

Both of our founders are immigrants. Mark arrived from Ukraine. Daria from China. They know what it means to be outside a system that wasn't designed for you. Learn with Mochi is a public-benefit corporation because the mission isn't to capture a market — it's to expand who gets to participate in the technological future. We have shipped to 62 countries across six continents. The product arrives as a blank slate so every child can create experiences in their own image.

A child is a scientist.

Not metaphorically. Literally. Children form hypotheses, test them, observe outcomes, and revise their understanding — naturally, instinctively, joyfully. The role of an educational tool is not to instruct but to create the conditions for this process to flourish. Our system is designed for open-ended play: there is no single "right answer," no score, no fail state. There is only what the child imagines, codes, and discovers for themselves.

Where we're going

A world where every child understands the technology that shapes their life.

Today, a few thousand engineers in a handful of cities decide how billions of people communicate, learn, work, and play. That concentration of creative power is unprecedented in human history — and it is unsustainable.

We are building toward a future where computational thinking is as universal as literacy. Where a child in rural Arkansas and a child in Nairobi both grow up knowing that technology is not magic — it is a language they can speak, a tool they can wield, a world they can shape.

Mochi is where that future begins: on a living room floor, with wooden blocks, a storybook, and a very curious bear.
